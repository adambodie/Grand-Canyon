Lipoflavonoid: What the heck is it?

by Jennifer Bodie



What is a lipoflavonoid you ask? It's a proprietary, over-the-counter, dietary supplement formula. Its purpose: To help improve circulation in the inner ear. What is the main thing it helps? Tinnitus, otherwise known as ringing in the ears.

But does it really stop tinnitus? Depends on who you ask. While the makers of lipoflavonoid say it does, the FDA does not approve lipoflavonoid expressly for this use. Though the FDA has no issue with its use; as it complies with FDA regulations, it does not have enough proof that lipoflavonoid is useful for stopping tinnitus. For example, studies at the Mayo Clinic found little evidence that it actually stops tinnitus, but lists it as an alternative therapy for tinnitus.

It's mainly made of eriodictyol glycoside, a bioflavonoid found naturally in lemon peels, along with multiple vitamins, including Vitamin B6 and B12, Vitamin C, Niacin, Riboflavin, Thiamine, Choline, Inositol and Pantothenic Acid.

So remember kiddies, if you have tinnitus and want to get rid of the ringing in the ear (or at least lessen it), try Lipoflavonoid!

Grand Canyon Formation

According to estimates, the Grand Canyon started developing 17 million years ago, when the Colorado Plateau started a long period of erosion that still occurs today

There are 19 distinct physiographic sections of the Grand Canyon, mainly from the Paleozoic and Proterozoic strata. These distinct sections are shown in the differing colors of the Grand Canyon.

The weather at the Grand Canyon varies depending on location. The South Rim, for example, gets considerably less snow than the North Rim. The Inner Gorge of the Grand Canyon is much warmer than the rims, with temperatures comparable to the deserts of Phoenix and Tucson. Monsoons are an occurring event in the Grand Canyon in late summer.
